# Peer references

> What the category looks like.
> What we lean toward (positive references) — and what we refuse to be (negative references).

---

## Positive references — the editorial / observational register

We want to feel like these brands. **Calm, considered, signed off on.**

### Editorial typography

- **The Atlantic** — running-head typography, restrained color, serif numerics in long-form sports writing
- **The New York Times** — the running-stat banners during US Open / Wimbledon coverage. Editorial seriousness applied to live sports data.
- **Bloomberg Terminal** — the *information density* (we don't copy this), but the **discipline** of mono-typography for system meta + serif for actual numbers
- **Yale University Press** — book-jacket typography, single-color disciplined wordmarks
- **The Economist** — editorial restraint, single-accent moments, calm color discipline

### Product UI we respect

- **Linear** — calm motion, restrained color, no ambient pulse, every animation is feedback
- **Vercel** — documentation typography, mono-caps for system meta, near-black UI without going neon
- **Stripe Atlas / Stripe marketing** — generous whitespace, serif accent moments, no hype copy
- **Apple Activity rings** — color discipline (one ring is one color; no traffic-light), single-accent moments
- **Robinhood (mobile)** — gentle palette discipline (muted pink for "down", not panic-red), big-number-as-hero pattern, the kinetic count-up

### Sports moments we draw from

- **The Athletic** — coach-voice writing, plain English, never hype
- **Football Manager match-day stat sheets** — dense data laid out without panic
- **MLB Statcast presentations** — court-as-frame for visualizations (their version of our court overlay)

## Negative references — what to avoid

We will never look like these. Even when the temptation is high.

### Sports analytics products that got the UX wrong

- **Hudl** — every screen is a dashboard with 14 widgets. The "kitchen sink in a web app." We refuse.
- **SwingVision (the UI)** — pro-grade jargon ("BP saved %", "kick spin variance"), spreadsheet density. We borrow their depth, refuse their interface.
- **PlaySight** — neon scoreboard energy, exclamation copy, courtside ad aesthetic
- **Synergy Sports** — power-user filter UIs, dropdown soup, configurable everything

### Generic SaaS / AI tropes

- **Gradient soup** — purple-to-pink-to-teal hero gradients. The 2022 AI startup look.
- **Neon green / radioactive yellow** — gamer-tech color systems. We use lime, not neon.
- **Helvetica 800 wordmarks** — generic, dated. We use Newsreader.
- **Glassmorphism / heavy backdrop blur** — we want clarity, not opacity.
- **Animated gradient borders / shimmer cards** — ambient motion is forbidden.
- **"Powered by AI" badges** — we don't announce the technology. We show what it does.

### Hype / urgency theater

- **Sports betting apps** — exclamation marks, urgency theater, "LIVE!!" everywhere
- **Crypto exchanges** — neon-on-black, panic-red price drops, hype-house copy
- **Generic productivity SaaS landing pages** — "Crush your", "Supercharge your", "Unleash the"

## Tennis-specific reference points

### Surface aesthetics (what tennis looks like in cultural memory)

- **Clay courts (Roland-Garros)** — terracotta dust, white lines, faded tradition. Our **light mode** lives here. Cream paper bg + clay accents.
- **Quiet locker room at night** — our **dark mode** lives here. Cream paper goes near-black, but the court stays green and the clay surface stays warm. The brand color story doesn't change with the lights, only its tone.
- **Wimbledon greens (grass courts)** — we don't reference this directly; lime is the *ball* color, not the surface

### Coaching culture references

- **Brad Gilbert's commentary** — specific, calm, observational. He'll say "she's losing the cross 64% on second serves" with no theater. That's our voice.
- **Patrick Mouratoglou's coaching academy materials** — clean diagram conventions, restrained color
- **Tennis Channel's broadcast graphics** — court-as-frame for serve placement. We borrow this convention.

### What tennis brands typically get wrong

- **USTA marketing** — generic stock photography, blue-and-yellow chevron energy
- **Local tennis academies** — "Champion Academy" energy, fake gold accents, action photography clichés
- **Tennis equipment brands (Wilson, Babolat)** — bombastic, gear-bro, color noise

We are *coaching*, not *equipment*. We are *editorial*, not *commerce*.

## College sports identity references

### Schools that get visual identity right

- **Stanford** (cardinal red + white discipline, serif Stanford wordmark)
- **Princeton** (orange + black, deep editorial heritage)
- **University of Chicago** (maroon, bookish, serious)
- **Yale** (navy + cream, never gimmicky)

### What we steal from college brand systems

- The **wordmark + crest** lockup pattern (we use wordmark + seam-checkmark)
- Single **accent color** + neutrals, never tri-color hero gradients
- **Editorial serifs** for institutional voice
- **Quiet confidence** — these schools don't need to shout

### What we don't steal

- Heraldic crests (we are modern, not 1450)
- Latin mottoes (we speak coach English)
- Athletic department mascot energy (we are the film room, not the locker room)

## Mood board summary

If a coach scrolled past three CourtCheck screenshots and three competitor screenshots, they should be able to tell us apart by:

1. **Cream paper background** instead of black-or-white-with-gradients
2. **Newsreader serif numbers** instead of sans numerics
3. **Italic-clay accent words** instead of bold-color emphasis
4. **Court-as-frame** on every dashboard tile
5. **No exclamation marks anywhere**
6. **Mono-caps "system voice"** for status indicators
7. **One lime moment per screen** instead of color noise

Those seven things are the brand. Everything else is supporting context.
